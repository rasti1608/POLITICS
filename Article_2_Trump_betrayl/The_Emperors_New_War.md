# The Emperor's New War

*When the Last Thread Snaps*

**By IronRUST** | March 18, 2026

---

I was never a true believer. I want to make that clear from the start, because what I'm about to say carries more weight if you understand where I stood before any of this happened.

I watched this man for years — the rallies, the promises, the patterns. I saw the exaggeration, the compulsive self-promotion, the way he would say one thing Tuesday and the opposite Thursday and dare you to notice. I recognized it because I grew up watching people like that operate in a different country, under a different system. I know what a con artist sounds like. I know how propaganda works. I spent the first twenty years of my life inside a machine that manufactured it.

So no — I never thought Donald Trump was going to save America. I never wore the hat. I never trusted the plan.

But in 2024, when the choice came down to him or a candidate who inspired even less confidence, I made a calculation that millions of others made too. Not the fanatics. Not the cult. Regular people who looked at the options, held their nose, and thought: maybe. When I saw who he was putting around himself — Tulsi Gabbard as Director of National Intelligence, a woman who left the Democratic Party over its addiction to regime change wars; voices who talked about restraint, about putting America first in more than just a slogan — I thought his own selfishness might be enough. A man that desperate to be great, that obsessed with legacy, wouldn't destroy himself. He wouldn't start the one war that would sink everything he built.

That was the thread I held onto. The thinnest thread in the room. Not belief. Not trust. Just the assumption that his ego would be the guardrail that his character never could be.

Three weeks into the war with Iran, that thread snapped. And if the thinnest thread in the room just broke — what does that tell you about yours?

---

## The Cracks Were Already There

The signs were visible long before March 2026 for anyone willing to see them.

June 2025. The twelve-day war with Iran. Israel pushed, Trump followed. Bombs fell on Iranian soil, and for twelve days the world held its breath. Then it stopped. At the time, you could still rationalize. Maybe it was a contained response. Maybe the people around him pulled him back. Maybe the system worked. The promise was cracked, but you could still argue it wasn't shattered.

But look at what was happening underneath. The people who were supposed to be guardrails — the voices of restraint inside the Pentagon and the National Security Council, people like Dan Caldwell and the America First cadre in the Department of Defense — were being systematically removed. Not through policy disagreements. Through phone calls. Through pressure from donors whose primary loyalty was to a foreign government, not to the country they claimed to serve. Laura Loomer could pick up the phone, call the president, and have an entire team of restraint-minded officials fired by morning. And she did. Joe Kent, the Green Beret who would later lead the National Counterterrorism Center, watched this process from the inside — the methodical replacement of everyone who might say "no" with someone who would say "yes." One by one, the guardrails were pulled from the road. And the people who thought their guys were in control of the administration discovered that their guys were outranked at every turn by a donor, a lobbyist, or a phone call from someone whose loyalty was to Tel Aviv, not to them.

And Trump let it happen. Not because he was overruled. Because he did not care enough to stop it. The promises about ending wars, draining the swamp, putting America first — they were campaign lines, not convictions. When the lobby came calling with enough money and enough flattery, the lines dissolved like they were written in smoke.

At CPAC in 2023, Trump stood on stage and declared: "We had a Republican party ruled by freaks, neocons, globalists, open border zealots, and fools. But we are never going back." The crowd roared. He promised to "demolish the deep state" and "expel the warmongers." He said it with conviction. He said it with fire. And every single person in that room believed it.

Standing beside Tucker Carlson before the 2024 election, Trump mocked Liz Cheney as a warmonger who wanted to send other people's children to die. "They're all warhawks when they're sitting in Washington in a nice building," he said. "Let's send ten thousand troops right into the mouth of the enemy." The contempt in his voice was real. Or at least it sounded real. And people voted based on it.

Remember all of that. Hold it in your mind. Because you are going to need it to understand what happened next.

## Three Weeks That Revealed Everything

March 2026. The United States and Israel launch a full-scale air campaign against Iran. No congressional authorization. No public debate. No catalyzing event — no Pearl Harbor, no September 11th. A war of choice, started on the advice of a foreign government whose prime minister publicly called it the fulfillment of a forty-year dream.

Within days, everything the administration promised would not happen, happened. Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz — the chokepoint through which twenty percent of the world's oil flows. Gas prices surged past levels not seen since before Trump took office. Diesel — the fuel that moves food, freight, and construction materials — spiked nearly a dollar in a single week. Oil hit a hundred dollars a barrel and kept climbing. The economic shock was not theoretical. It arrived while the administration was still claiming victory.

And then came the developments that stripped away every remaining illusion.

The man who stood at CPAC and promised to "expel the warmongers" sat down and wrote a thousand-word tribute to Mark Levin — a man who is, by any honest measure, the purest distillation of warmongering neoconservatism in American media. Not a populist. Not a nationalist. Not someone who has ever placed American interests above those of Israel. Trump declared that Levin is the standard bearer of MAGA. That if you criticize Levin, you are not MAGA. That bombing Iran *is* MAGA.

The man who mocked Liz Cheney for wanting to "send ten thousand troops into the mouth of the enemy" deployed 2,500 Marines toward Kharg Island — a flat oil terminal in the Persian Gulf, within range of every Iranian missile, drone, and mine. A member of Congress went on television and argued, with a straight face, that Marines standing on Iranian territory under Iranian fire would not constitute "boots on the ground" because technically it is an island.

The man who said "MAGA means no more endless wars" expelled Tucker Carlson from the movement for questioning the war. Expelled Megyn Kelly. Drove Marjorie Taylor Greene from Congress. Targeted Thomas Massie — one of the last principled voices in the Republican caucus — for removal, funded by Israeli billionaire money. The people who actually believed the promises are being purged. The people whose ideology Trump swore to destroy are writing his talking points.

And when confronted with the contradiction — when asked how MAGA can mean bombing Iran when it was supposed to mean the opposite — Trump answered with the only honest thing he has said in months: "I am MAGA."

He is right about that. MAGA is whatever he says it is on any given morning. It has no principles that survive past his next Truth Social post. It is not a movement. It is a mirror that reflects one man's impulses back at a country that wanted to see something more.

## The Resignation That Said Everything

On March 17, 2026, Joe Kent resigned as the Director of the National Counterterrorism Center.

This is not a podcast host with an opinion. This is not a columnist filing a take. This is a Green Beret who deployed to combat eleven times. A Gold Star husband whose wife, Shannon, was killed in a war that he now says was "manufactured by Israel." A man confirmed by the United States Senate, 52 to 44, to lead the agency responsible for analyzing and detecting terrorist threats against this country.

His resignation letter was precise and devastating.

"Iran posed no imminent threat to our nation," he wrote. "And it is clear that we started this war due to pressure from Israel and its powerful American lobby."

He addressed Trump directly: "In your first administration, you understood better than any modern president how to decisively apply military power without getting us drawn into never-ending wars." Then he said what needed to be said: "Early in this administration, high-ranking Israeli officials and influential members of the American media deployed a misinformation campaign that wholly undermined your America First platform and sowed pro-war sentiments to encourage a war with Iran."

He did not call the president stupid. He did not call him evil. He told him, plainly and with respect: you were conned. The same people who dragged America into Iraq did it to you with Iran. Same playbook. Same lies. Same result.

And Trump's response? "I always thought he was a nice guy, but I always thought he was weak on security, very weak on security." He added: "When somebody is working with us that says they didn't think Iran was a threat — we don't want those people. They're not smart people."

A Gold Star husband. Eleven deployments. The man who sat in the intelligence briefings. Who saw the classified assessments. Who knows what the war planning looks like from the inside. And the president's verdict: not smart. Not savvy. Not wanted.

The administration's surrogates piled on within hours. A deputy called him "a crazed egomaniac" and "a loser." Anonymous officials leaked that he was "a known leaker" — an accusation delivered, with no apparent sense of irony, by an anonymous leaker. They said he had been "cut out of briefings months ago," as if being excluded from the planning of a catastrophe is a mark of shame rather than a badge of honor.

This is what happens now to anyone inside the system who says what is true. Not what is politically convenient. Not what the donors want to hear. What is actually, demonstrably, verifiably true: that this war was started under pressure from a foreign government and its lobby, that it serves no American interest, and that the people who launched it have no idea how to end it.

The message to every general, every analyst, every official who privately agrees with Kent is unmistakable: speak up and we will destroy you. Keep quiet and you keep your career. That is the choice. And in a system that operates on that logic, the truth doesn't just become difficult to say. It becomes dangerous.

And then there is Tulsi Gabbard. Kent's boss. The Director of National Intelligence. The woman who built her entire political identity on opposing regime change wars. Who left the Democratic Party over it. Who stood on a debate stage and dismantled Kamala Harris. Who called out the Clinton war machine by name, to its face, and didn't flinch. Who went to Syria herself to see the truth on the ground.

I believed in her. In 2020, I supported her presidential campaign — shook her hand, followed every speech, believed that here was someone who actually meant it when she said no more regime change wars. She was one of the reasons I thought the people around Trump in his second term might be different. If Tulsi was in the room, maybe the worst impulses would be checked. That was not a small part of why I allowed myself to hope.

And now her counterterrorism director just resigned and told the country that this war was built on Israeli pressure and American lies. The White House told her to fire Kent before he could resign. She refused — but she also did not resign herself. She has not spoken publicly. She has not confirmed or denied what Kent said.

Tulsi — where are you? The woman who stared down Hillary Clinton and called her the queen of warmongers. The woman who sacrificed her career in the Democratic Party because she refused to stay silent about endless wars. Where is that person right now? Because the war you spent your entire public life warning against is happening on your watch, under your authority, and your silence is deafening.

If Tulsi Gabbard remains in this administration after what Kent revealed, then every speech she ever gave about opposing these wars, every principle she ever claimed to hold, every reason anyone ever believed she was different — all of it was performance. And the last guardrail was never a guardrail at all. It was a prop.

## The Danger Underneath

I wrote two weeks ago, in an article called *"The Danger No One Is Talking About,"* that the United States had entered a war with no defined objective, no exit strategy, no realistic path to victory, and an escalation dynamic that favors Iran. Everything that has happened since has confirmed that assessment and made it worse.

Iran still controls the Strait of Hormuz. Countries are negotiating passage directly with Tehran — not in dollars, but in Chinese yuan. That is not a temporary arrangement. That is the architecture of a post-dollar world being built in real time, made possible by American stupidity.

Every ally Trump has asked for help has refused. Germany: "This is not our war." France: defensive posture only. Italy: "We are not involved." Poland: ruled it out. Japan: no. Australia: no. The United Kingdom: noncommittal at best. Not one country in the world is willing to send its navy into the Strait to bail out a president who started a war without asking anyone and is now begging everyone.

Israel's interceptor supply is critically depleted. Iran's drone and missile production continues from dispersed facilities, many underground, many in the eastern part of the country that remains beyond the reach of current strikes. Iranian officials have stated publicly that they have not yet fired missiles produced in the last decade — that they are using older inventory first, saving advanced weapons for later, when defenses are further degraded. That is not desperation. That is strategy.

More than two hundred American service members have been wounded across seven countries. Trump is privately discussing ground troops inside Iran. The Marine Expeditionary Units are heading toward Kharg Island — a target that Iran has spent forty years preparing to defend.

And the person making these decisions is someone whose own allies, after meeting him face to face, have privately described as "dangerous" and "out of his mind." Not critics. Not political opponents. Leaders who wanted to support him, who left their meetings frightened.

This is the danger that sits beneath all the other dangers. It is not just that the war has no exit. It is that the man who would need to find one may not be capable of understanding why he needs one. He is still talking about the Strait of Hormuz being "in great shape." He is still comparing Iran to Venezuela. He is still claiming that the war is essentially won while Iranian missiles strike targets across the Gulf and tankers burn in the waterway he says is under control.

The people who tried to be guardrails have been removed. Tucker Carlson — expelled from MAGA and now facing a CIA criminal referral for the crime of talking to Iranians, which is what journalists do. Thomas Massie — targeted for removal from Congress by foreign-aligned donors. Joe Kent — resigned, smeared, and discarded within hours. The only voices left in the room are the ones who wanted this war, the ones who profit from it, and the ones too afraid to say otherwise.

## The Clothes Were Never There

I know how this feels. Not because I was a true believer who lost his faith. But because I have felt this kind of collapse before — the moment when something you allowed yourself to hope for reveals itself as hollow.

I felt it as a young man in Slovakia, watching a political system that promised equality and justice operate as a machine of surveillance and lies. I felt it coming to America at twenty years old, unable to speak the language, carrying nothing but the belief that this country was different — that here, the government served the people instead of the other way around. I have spent thirty years slowly, painfully, discovering the distance between that promise and this reality.

And I feel it now. Watching a man who said all the right things — who had the platform, the mandate, the moment — choose Mark Levin over Tucker Carlson. Choose Laura Loomer over Joe Kent. Choose a foreign prime minister's forty-year dream over every American soldier, trucker, farmer, and family being crushed by a war that serves no American interest and answers no American need.

For those who supported him with conviction — who went to the rallies, who wore the hat, who argued with friends and family, who put their credibility on the line — I am not here to say I told you so. That is not what this is. You were not stupid. You were not gullible. You heard a man promise to end the wars, drain the swamp, and put your country first, and you believed him because those things needed to happen and no one else was saying them. The betrayal is not yours. It is his.

But the betrayal is now undeniable. And the most dangerous thing any of us can do — more dangerous than the war itself — is to keep pretending otherwise.

There is no five-dimensional chess. There is no secret plan. There is no team of brilliant strategists working behind the scenes to turn this disaster into a triumph. There is a man who believed he could topple Iran the way he captured Maduro in Venezuela — quickly, cheaply, triumphantly — and who is now discovering, in real time, that he was lied to by people whose interests were never his and whose loyalty was never to America. And instead of confronting that reality, he is doubling down, attacking the people who warned him, embracing the people who deceived him, and sending Marines toward a kill zone that Iran has spent four decades preparing to defend.

The emperor has no clothes. He never did. Some of us suspected it from the beginning and held onto a thread anyway — the thread of self-interest, the thread of ego, the thread of "surely he won't be *this* reckless." That thread is gone. It snapped under the weight of a war that every credible voice warned against, that his own military advised against, that his own counterterrorism chief resigned over, and that not a single ally on earth is willing to support.

If you still see clothes — if you are still constructing explanations for why this is secretly going well, why the chaos is actually strategy, why the man writing love letters to Mark Levin while two hundred soldiers lie wounded is actually playing the deepest game of all — then you are not looking at him. You are looking at the version of him you built in your head. And that version does not exist. It never did.

The war revealed it. But the truth was always there, underneath the rallies and the slogans and the hats, waiting for a crisis big enough to strip everything else away.

This is that crisis. And the clothes were never there.
