# The Village of Idiots

*Kocurkovo With Nuclear Weapons*

**By IronRUST** | March 23, 2026

---

In Slovak, there is a word — *kocurkovo*. It describes a place run by fools. A village where the leaders are so incompetent, so detached from reality, that everything they do makes things worse, and yet they strut around convinced they are geniuses. Every Slovak knows the word. Every Slovak has laughed at the stories. Nobody ever imagined it would describe the White House during a war.

We are living in kocurkovo now. Except the village idiots have aircraft carriers, nuclear codes, and the power to set the global economy on fire. And nobody is laughing.

## Twenty-Six Hours

On Friday, March 20th, at 5:13 PM, the President of the United States posted on Truth Social that the Strait of Hormuz was not America's problem. Other countries should handle it. "The United States does not" use the Strait, he wrote. Whatever needed to be done would be "an easy Military Operation for them."

Twenty-six hours later, at 7:44 PM on Saturday, March 21st, the same man posted this: "If Iran doesn't FULLY OPEN, WITHOUT THREAT, the Strait of Hormuz, within 48 HOURS from this exact point in time, the United States of America will hit and obliterate their various POWER PLANTS, STARTING WITH THE BIGGEST ONE FIRST."

From "not our problem" to threatening a war crime — the deliberate destruction of civilian power infrastructure, prohibited under the Geneva Conventions that America signed nearly ninety years ago — in just over a day. Senator Ed Markey called it what it is: "Trump has no plan to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, so he is threatening to attack Iran's civil power plants. This would be a war crime." Senator Chris Murphy was more blunt: "He's lost control of the war and he is panicking."

And then, before the 48 hours expired, another reversal. Monday morning, 7:00 AM — precisely timed for the opening of financial markets — Trump posted that the United States and Iran had been engaged in "very good and productive conversations" about "a complete and total resolution." He announced a five-day postponement of the power plant strikes.

Oil prices dropped immediately. Stock futures surged. Gold pulled back. The markets exhaled.

Within hours, Iran's Foreign Ministry issued a flat denial. "No negotiations have been held with the United States. Fake news is used to manipulate the financial and oil markets and escape the quagmire in which the US and Israel are trapped."

Three positions in seventy-two hours. Not our problem. We will obliterate you. Actually, we are having wonderful talks. Each one contradicting the last. Each one delivered with total confidence. Each one treated by the man who issued it as though the previous one never existed.

This is kocurkovo. This is the village of idiots. And they are running a war.

## The Five-Day Window

Ask yourself a simple question: why five days?

If the peace talks are real — if Trump and Iran are genuinely negotiating a resolution — why set a five-day clock? Why not announce an open-ended pause while diplomacy proceeds?

Because the five days are not about diplomacy. They are about positioning.

Five days from Monday morning is Friday evening. Markets close on Friday. The weekend arrives. And right now, two Marine Expeditionary Units — between 2,500 and 5,000 Marines — are converging on the Persian Gulf region. Military analysts tracking the buildup estimate they will be in position by the end of the week.

The pattern is not subtle. Calm the markets for five trading days with talk of peace. Let oil prices settle. Let the stock market stabilize. Let the American public take a breath. And then, over the weekend — when markets are closed and the news cycle is at its weakest — launch the operation. Kharg Island. Or Qeshm Island. Or the naval facility at Konarak. Somewhere that Iran has spent forty years preparing to defend.

By Monday morning, it will be a fait accompli. The Marines will either have taken their objective — in which case Trump declares victory and the market shock is absorbed — or they will be pinned down under drone and missile fire in a replay of Gallipoli, and the world wakes up to a catastrophe that cannot be undone.

Senior American officials have reportedly told Israeli counterparts in recent days that a ground operation to capture Kharg Island may be unavoidable. Lindsey Graham went on Fox News and compared it to the Battle of Iwo Jima — a battle that killed 7,000 Americans and wounded 19,000 — and said it approvingly. As if the slaughter of American Marines is something to aspire to rather than prevent.

This is the play. The peace talks are the cover. The five days are the countdown. Not to diplomacy. To the next escalation.

## The Apprentice Runs a War

Professor Jeffrey Sachs, watching Trump's public statements on Monday, said what needed to be said. "If this turns out to be completely false, I think we have to consider the possibility that the president has really lost touch with reality. Something is seriously wrong."

Sachs did not use those words lightly. He is one of the most respected economists and foreign policy voices in the world. He has advised governments across decades. And he watched the president claim successful negotiations that the other party says never happened, promise joint control of the Strait of Hormuz with "me and the Ayatollah, whoever the Ayatollah is," and compare this war to Venezuela — a country that bears no resemblance to Iran in military capability, population, geography, or strategic significance.

Sachs used a clinical term: confabulation. It means the brain filling in gaps with invented narratives that the person genuinely believes to be real. It is different from lying. A liar knows the truth and conceals it. A person who confabulates does not know the truth anymore. They have constructed a reality that exists only in their own mind, and they cannot distinguish it from the world outside.

Whether or not that clinical assessment is accurate — and time will tell — the observable facts are damning on their own. The president claimed talks that Iran explicitly denies. He cannot name who he is negotiating with because, he says, "I don't want him to be killed" — killed by Israel, his own ally in the war. His partner Netanyahu publicly announced, on the same day Trump claimed peace talks, that Israel will continue bombing Iran and Lebanon and targeting Iranian scientists — adding only that Israel would "safeguard our vital interests in any agreement." And Trump's response to all of this was to praise the "very productive conversations" and suggest that the war is essentially over.

Sachs put it plainly: "What we're seeing is not only an unconstitutional assertion of power. We're seeing a complete and utter breakdown of competence — of normal process, even of rational review of options." He noted that this collapse extends far beyond the war. The tariff chaos, the government shutdowns, the economic upheaval — none of it reflects any discernible process of deliberation, expertise, or consequence analysis. It is governance by impulse, executed by a man surrounded by people who either share his detachment from reality or profit from it.

And then Sachs asked the question that should be echoing through every corridor in Washington: "Are there any grown-ups left? Is there a single senator in the Republican party watching this and thinking that the state of our republic and the world are on the line?"

The answer, so far, has been silence. One senator — Rand Paul — has spoken. The rest have watched a president threaten war crimes, claim negotiations that don't exist, and deploy Marines toward a potential massacre, and said nothing. Their oath is to defend the Constitution. The Constitution gives Congress — and only Congress — the power to declare war. They have abandoned that responsibility entirely, and their silence is complicity.

## The Three-Sided Trap

The most dangerous feature of this crisis is that none of the three parties involved can stop.

Iran cannot stop because stopping means surrendering the only leverage it has ever possessed. After the nuclear deal was torn up, after their leaders were assassinated during negotiations, after 15,000 strikes on their country — what guarantee would be worth anything? The Iranian security establishment believes their greatest mistake was agreeing to a ceasefire after the twelve-day war in June 2025. The cautious Ayatollah who made that call is dead — killed by this war. His replacements have concluded, based on bitter experience, that any pause is simply an opportunity for the United States and Israel to rearm and attack again. Their position is explicit: this must be the last war. The cost must be so high that neither the US nor Israel ever considers doing this again. That requires sustained pain, not a premature deal based on promises from a country that has broken every promise it has ever made to them.

Trump cannot stop because stopping means admitting failure. His entire political identity is built on winning, on strength, on never backing down. He entered this war believing it would be Venezuela — a quick decapitation, a regime collapse, a triumphant declaration. Three and a half weeks later, Iran still controls the Strait, still fires missiles, still demonstrates capabilities the administration claimed were destroyed. Every press conference contradicts the last. Every claim of victory is undercut by the reality on the ground. And as Trita Parsi warned, Trump has a shrinking window in which he can still construct a narrative of success. The longer this goes, the more obvious the disaster becomes, and the harder it gets to claim victory even to his own base. At that point, his incentives to exit may actually decrease — because the only alternative to admitting defeat is escalation.

And Israel will not stop because permanent war is the strategy. Netanyahu publicly stated — on the same day Trump claimed peace talks — that Israel will continue bombing Iran, continue bombing Lebanon, and continue targeting Iranian scientists. Finance Minister Smotrich announced plans to annex southern Lebanon. The Greater Israel project does not have a pause button. Every moderate voice that might have negotiated — on the Iranian side or the Israeli side — has been systematically eliminated. And even if Trump genuinely tried to restrain Israel, the donor structure, the lobbying apparatus, and the institutional capture of American foreign policy by Israeli interests would prevent it. AIPAC's influence doesn't take a day off because the president posts about peace on Truth Social.

This is the trap. Three actors, none of whom can stop, locked in an escalation dynamic that has no natural exit point. Iran won't stop until the pain is severe enough to guarantee it never happens again. Trump can't stop without political self-destruction. And Israel won't stop because the war is the plan — not a problem to be solved, but the objective itself.

And any agreement — even a real one, even one made in good faith by all parties — would be meaningless. Because what structural guarantee can America offer after the nuclear deal? What promise survives the next president, or the next tweet? And what deal can hold when one of the three parties — Israel — has demonstrated for decades that it will undermine any agreement that constrains its expansion? Every ceasefire in the region's history was violated by someone. Usually the same someone. And everyone at the table knows it.

## The Trap Within the Trap

Here is what almost no one is saying, and what terrifies me more than anything else in this crisis.

If the five-day window is what it appears to be — a staging period for a Marine landing on Kharg Island or another Iranian-controlled position — then the most likely outcome is not an American victory. It is an American disaster. Military analysts have compared it to Gallipoli. Marines landing on a flat island within range of Iranian missiles, drones, mines, and coastal defenses. Iran has over 80,000 Shahed drones — possibly more, because they have been dispersed so effectively that even intelligence agencies have lost count. These drones cost less than ten thousand dollars each. The interceptors that would defend the Marines cost over a million dollars per shot. The math is the same math that has defined this entire war, except now it will be applied not to ships and bases but to human beings standing on exposed ground.

And if that landing fails — if Marines die in significant numbers on an island that Iran has spent forty years preparing to defend — the pressure to escalate will become overwhelming. Not from Iran. From within. From Lindsey Graham, who already compared this to Iwo Jima. From the neocon establishment that pushed for this war. From an administration that cannot survive the political fallout of a visible military humiliation.

And this is where Israel enters the darkest calculation. Military analysts and Israeli contacts have suggested that if an American ground operation fails, Israel may conclude that the conventional path has been exhausted — and that the only remaining option is nuclear weapons. Not because it makes strategic sense. Because a state that defines every setback as existential, led by people who have shown zero restraint in Gaza and Lebanon, operating under a political structure that cannot survive admitting defeat, is capable of making decisions that would reshape the world permanently.

This is not speculation from the fringe. This is the logical endpoint of the escalation ladder that this war has been climbing since day one. And the man at the top of that ladder — the man who would need to prevent the worst outcome — is posting about peace talks that don't exist, comparing Iran to Venezuela, and threatening war crimes between rounds of golf.

## How We Got Here

This is where the anger gives way to something colder. Because the hardest question is not "how do we get out of this?" The hardest question is: how did the most powerful country in the history of the world end up with this?

The answer is not Donald Trump. Trump is a symptom. The disease is older and deeper.

It is a political system so corroded by money that a foreign government's lobby can purchase American foreign policy outright — and anyone who says so is called an antisemite. It is a media landscape so consolidated and so captured that six corporations can manufacture consent for wars that serve no public interest, and the journalists who resist are threatened with prosecution. It is a culture so saturated with propaganda that millions of people will watch a president contradict himself three times in seventy-two hours and call it strategy. It is an electoral system so broken that the choice in 2024 was between two candidates that most of the country did not want — and the one who won did so by promising the exact opposite of what he has delivered.

I wrote about this in a song once. I called the album "The System Burns." Track by track, it documents the machinery: the wars fought for profit, the media that sells them, the lobbies that buy the politicians, the pharmaceutical companies that poison the food supply, the intelligence agencies that surveil the citizens, the financial system that bails out the rich and bankrupts the rest. Every track traces a different symptom back to the same disease: money has captured every institution that was supposed to protect the public from exactly this kind of catastrophe.

And now the catastrophe is here. Not as a warning. Not as a forecast. As a twenty-four-hour news cycle in which the president threatens war crimes, claims peace talks that don't exist, deploys Marines toward a kill zone, and posts about it all on the same social media platform where he sells digital trading cards of himself.

Professor Sachs is right. This is a breakdown of competence. But it is also a breakdown of the system that was supposed to prevent incompetence from reaching the levers of ultimate power. The guardrails were supposed to hold. The Constitution was supposed to matter. Congress was supposed to check the executive. The intelligence community was supposed to inform, not be overruled by a son-in-law with real estate debts and a foreign government's agenda. The media was supposed to question, not amplify. The military was supposed to advise, not be ignored.

All of it failed. Not because the structures don't exist. Because the structures were hollowed out by decades of corruption, captured by money, and finally handed to a man whose only qualification for the job was his ability to perform confidence on television.

## The Warning

I am not a military analyst. I am not a senator. I am not an intelligence official. I am a man from a small village in eastern Slovakia who came to America thirty years ago because he believed in a promise, and who has spent the last three decades watching that promise erode.

But I can read a calendar. And what the calendar says is this:

Five days from Monday is Friday. Markets close Friday afternoon. Marines arrive by the end of the week. The "peace talks" that Iran says don't exist will have produced nothing, because there is nothing to produce. And then the weekend comes. And with it, whatever this administration has been planning behind the smoke screen of diplomacy that was never diplomacy at all.

If what comes next is what the evidence suggests — a ground operation against an Iranian position, launched under cover of a fake negotiation pause — then we are not looking at an off-ramp. We are looking at the next step on the escalation ladder. And the step after that may not be one that anyone can walk back from.

Every American with a family member in uniform should be paying attention right now. Every citizen who pays taxes that fund this war should be paying attention. Every person on earth who buys gasoline, eats food that was transported by diesel, or lives in a country whose economy depends on energy that flows through the Strait of Hormuz — which is everyone — should be paying attention.

Because the village idiots are about to make their next move. And the village is the whole world.
